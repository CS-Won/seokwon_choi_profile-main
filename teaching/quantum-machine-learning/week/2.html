Week 2 · Quantum machine learning

Spring 2025 · QIYA — Lecturer: Seokwon Choi

What does "quantum machine learning" actually mean?

On near-term hardware, "QML" almost always refers to the variational quantum algorithm (VQA) family (Cerezo et al., 2021). The picture is short enough to memorize:

  1. Encode the input data into a quantum state.
  2. Run a parametrized quantum circuit U(θ) — the "quantum model".
  3. Measure some observable to get a real number.
  4. Send that number to a classical optimizer and update θ.

Repeat steps 2–4 until the loss settles. That's it. The diagram below is essentially the entire NISQ-era playbook.

Where does "variational" come from?

The word is borrowed from the variational method in quantum mechanics. There, to estimate the ground-state energy of a Hamiltonian H, you pick a family of trial states ∣ψ(θ)⟩ and minimize the Rayleigh quotient

E(θ)=⟨ψ(θ)∣ψ(θ)⟩⟨ψ(θ)∣H∣ψ(θ)⟩​≥E0​,

with E0​ the true ground-state energy. The upper-bound property of the right-hand side gives you a clean optimization target.

QML borrows the recipe wholesale. The trial state is now ∣ψ(θ)⟩=U(θ)∣ϕ(x)⟩where ∣ϕ(x)⟩ is the encoding of the data, and the loss is an expectation value

L(θ)=⟨ψ(θ)∣O∣ψ(θ)⟩

for some Hermitian observable O we picked to encode the task (for example, a Pauli-Z on the output qubit for binary classification).

The three building blocks, written out

1. Encoding map E

A map from classical data to a quantum state,

E:Rd→H,x↦∣ϕ(x)⟩.

We will spend all of week 3 on the three standard choices (basis, amplitude, angle). For now just remember that this map is fixed once the encoder is chosen — it has no trainable parameters.

2. Ansatz U(θ)

A unitary built from a few simple ingredients we know how to run on hardware:

U(θ)=ℓ=1∏L​(Wℓ​⋅j⨂​RPj​​(θℓ,j​)),

where each RPj​​(θ)=exp(−iθPj​/2) is a single-qubit rotation around a Pauli Pj​∈{X,Y,Z}, and Wℓ​ is a fixed entangling block (a layer of CNOTs, for example). L is the number of layers — depth is the most important hyperparameter you choose.

3. Measurement

Pick an observable and estimate its expectation by repeating the circuit many times (shots):

⟨O⟩​(θ)=S1​s=1∑S​os​,Var=SVar(O)​.

The square-root scaling is just the central limit theorem: to halve the statistical error you need four times as many shots. That cost is what makes "cheap" loss evaluations on QPUs not actually cheap.

Putting it together: a tiny example

Suppose we want to do binary classification on a 1-D feature x∈R with label y∈{+1,−1}. Choose:

  • encoder ∣ϕ(x)⟩=Ry​(x)∣0⟩,
  • ansatz U(θ)=Rz​(θ2​)Ry​(θ1​),
  • observable O=Z, and read off the predicted label as sign(⟨Z⟩).

The loss for one example is

ℓ(θ;x,y)=(y−⟨0∣V†(θ,x)ZV(θ,x)∣0⟩)2,

with V(θ,x)=U(θ)Ry​(x). We run the device many shots to estimate the expectation, plug into the loss, and update θ with the classical optimizer. This minimal three-gate model is already the entire VQA skeleton — bigger problems just add layers and qubits.

What can go right (and wrong)?

VQAs are popular because the circuits are shallow, so they are realistic on NISQ devices, and because the classical optimizer absorbs much of the noise. But two warnings show up everywhere:

  • Barren plateaus. For random deep ansatzes the gradient variance can shrink as Var(∂θ​L)∼2−n, making training exponentially hard in the qubit count n (McClean et al., 2018). Mitigations: structured ansatzes, smart parameter initialization, layer-wise training.
  • Shot noise. Every gradient estimate has finite-shot variance. Optimizers that handle stochastic gradients (Adam, SPSA) are the rule.

We will see the practical training algorithm — the parameter-shift rule — in week 5, after we cover the encoding step in week 3.
