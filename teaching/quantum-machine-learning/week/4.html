Week 4 · Neural networks

Spring 2025 · QIYA — Lecturer: Seokwon Choi

Classical neural networks, in plain words

Before we touch the quantum version next week, let's nail down what a classical neural network actually is. The mental picture is simple: a network is a big function fθ​:Rd→Rk built by stacking tiny, identical pieces called neurons. The parameters θ are just the weights and biases inside those neurons, and training adjusts them with the gradient-descent loop from Week 1.

The perceptron — one neuron

The atomic unit of a feed-forward network is the perceptron. Given an input vector x∈Rd, a weight vector w∈Rd, a scalar bias b, and a nonlinearity σ, the neuron computes

a(x)=σ(w⊤x+b)=σ(i=1∑d​wi​xi​+b).

Two steps, very explicitly: (1) take a weighted sum of the inputs and shift by a bias — this is the "affine" or "pre-activation" part; (2) push the result through σ to get the final activation a. Without σ, no matter how many layers we stack the whole network would collapse back to a single linear map — the nonlinearity is what gives neural nets their power.

Choosing the nonlinearity

A handful of activation functions cover almost everything in practice. The three classics:

  • Sigmoid: σ(x)=1+e−x1​. Maps into (0,1), smooth, but saturates for large ∣x∣ (the derivative goes to zero, which slows training in deep networks).
  • tanh: σ(x)=tanh(x). Same S-shape but zero-centred in (−1,1). Often nicer for intermediate layers than sigmoid.
  • ReLU: σ(x)=max(0,x). Cheap, non-saturating on the positive side, and now the default in modern deep learning.

For a binary classification output you usually keep a sigmoid (so the output reads as a probability); for multi-class outputs the final layer is a softmax. Inside the network, ReLU and its variants dominate.

From one neuron to a layer

A single perceptron can only represent very simple decision rules. A whole layer is just many neurons computed in parallel from the same input. Stack their weights into a matrix and we can write the layer in one line of linear algebra. If layer ℓ has nℓ​ neurons and the previous layer produced a(ℓ−1)∈Rnℓ−1​, the layer's output is

a(ℓ)=σ(W(ℓ)a(ℓ−1)+b(ℓ)),

where W(ℓ)∈Rnℓ​×nℓ−1​ is the weight matrix, b(ℓ)∈Rnℓ​ is the bias vector, and σ is applied element-wise. Every entry of a(ℓ) is exactly one perceptron of the form above.

Feed-forward: one layer at a time

A feed-forward neural network is just a chain of such layers, evaluated strictly left-to-right (no loops, no shortcuts back to earlier layers). Set the input layer to the data, a(0)=x, then for ℓ=1,2,…,L compute

z(ℓ)=W(ℓ)a(ℓ−1)+b(ℓ),a(ℓ)=σ(z(ℓ)).

The final activation a(L) is the network's prediction y^​. The whole network as one function is just the composition

fθ​(x)=σ(W(L)σ(W(L−1)⋯σ(W(1)x+b(1))⋯)),

with parameter set θ={W(ℓ),b(ℓ)}ℓ=1L​. That is, quite literally, the entire definition of a feed-forward network. Everything fancy you have seen — convolutional layers, residual connections, attention — is some specialization of choosing the weight matrices or wiring.

How does it learn? (a one-paragraph reminder)

We already saw the answer in Week 1: pick a loss L(θ)=N1​∑i​ℓ(fθ​(xi​),yi​) and run gradient descent. The only new ingredient inside a network is how to compute ∇θ​L efficiently — that is the backpropagation algorithm, which applies the chain rule layer-by-layer from output back to input. The per-step update for any weight is the same one we wrote in Week 1,

W(ℓ)←W(ℓ)−η∂W(ℓ)∂L​.

We will not re-derive backprop in this seminar — it is standard material in any deep-learning course. The thing to remember is that training a classical NN ultimately just needs two ingredients: a way to evaluate the network (the feed-forward pass above) and a way to compute gradients (backprop).

Why is this the setup for QML?

Next week we will replace the feed-forward chain with a parametrized quantum circuit, and the layer-by-layer affine map W(ℓ)a(ℓ−1)+b(ℓ) will become a layer of trainable rotation gates. The nonlinearity σ will sneak in through measurement — squaring amplitudes is not linear. And because backprop does not work on a real quantum device (it would require storing intermediate quantum states), we will need a different way to compute ∇θ​L: the parameter-shift rule. That is the entire content of Week 5.
