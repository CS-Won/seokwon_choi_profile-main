Week 4 · Midterm — worked practice problems

Spring 2025 · Yonsei PHY3101 — Tutor: Seokwon Choi

Midterm: full worked solutions

These are the four problems from PHY3101 — Quantum Mechanics I, Spring 2024 Midterm. We work each one through carefully, paying attention to the boundary conditions and the matching trick at potential jumps. Make sure you can reproduce the algebra unaided before exam day.

Q1. Wells of various shapes (30 pts)

Q1 (i) Infinite square well, ∣x∣<a

Inside the well V=0; outside V=∞, so ψ(±a)=0. The TISE is

−2mℏ2​ψ′′=Eψ,ψ′′=−k2ψ,k=2mE​/ℏ.

Use the symmetry of the well (even potential ⇒ states come in even and odd parity).

  • Even: ψ(x)=Acos(kx), boundary cos(ka)=0 ⇒ kn​a=(2n−1)π/2, n=1,2,….
  • Odd: ψ(x)=Asin(kx), boundary sin(ka)=0 ⇒ kn​a=nπ.

Combining and re-indexing,

ψn​(x)=a​1​sin(2anπ(x+a)​),En​=8ma2n2π2ℏ2​,n=1,2,3,…​

Q1 (ii) Finite square well, ∣x∣<a

Now V=−V0​ inside and V=0 outside. Look for bound states −V0​<E<0. Define

k=2m(E+V0​)​/ℏ(inside),κ=−2mE​/ℏ(outside).

By parity the bound states split into:

  • Even: ψin​=Acos(kx), ψout​=Be−κ∣x∣. Continuity of ψ and ψ′ at x=a gives ktan(ka)=κ.
  • Odd: ψin​=Asin(kx), −kcot(ka)=κ.

Eliminating E via k2+κ2=2mV0​/ℏ2≡z02​/a2 and writing z=ka reduces the quantization condition to a single transcendental equation in z:

ztanz=z02​−z2​(even),−zcotz=z02​−z2​(odd).

Each solution determines E=−V0​+ℏ2k2/(2m). A finite well has only a finite number of bound states, but always at least one even state (no matter how shallow).

Q1 (iii) Asymmetric well: V=∞(x<0),V0​(0<x<a),0(x>a)

An infinite wall at x=0 kills ψ(0), then there is a barrier of height V0​ from 0 to a, then free space. Bound states need E<V0​ so that ψ decays beyond x=a.

Define

κ=2m(V0​−E)​/ℏ(0<x<a),k=2mE​/ℏ(x>a).

Wait — for a true bound state we need decay outside, so we actually need E<0 for x>a to give an exponential. Set q=−2mE​/ℏ. Then:

ψ(x)=⎩⎨⎧​0,Asinh(κx)+Bcosh(κx),Ce−qx,​x<0,0<x<a,x>a.​

The wall at x=0 forces B=0. Matching ψ and ψ′ at x=a yields

Asinh(κa)=Ce−qa,Aκcosh(κa)=−qCe−qa.

Dividing,

κcoth(κa)=−q.

Combined with κ2+q2=2mV0​/ℏ2, this is the transcendental quantization condition. (The minus sign means no bound state exists unless the well-and-wall combination is deep enough — a standard threshold phenomenon.)

Q2. Step potential (20 pts)

V(x)=0 for x≤0 and V(x)=V0​ for x>0. Incoming wave from the left.

(a) E<V0​

Region I (x≤0): ψI​=Aeikx+Be−ikx,k=2mE​/ℏ. Region II (x>0): ψII​=Ce−κx,κ=2m(V0​−E)​/ℏ. Matching ψ and ψ′ at x=0:

A+B=C,ik(A−B)=−κC.

Solve for B:

AB​=ik−κik+κ​⟹R=​AB​​2=k2+κ2k2+κ2​=1.

Total reflection: the wave penetrates a bit into the wall (over a length ∼1/κ) but is fully sent back. Classically you would just bounce off; quantum mechanics agrees on the probability flux but disagrees on what happens at the boundary.

(b) E>V0​: reflection coefficient

Now Region II also propagates: ψII​=Feik′x,k′=2m(E−V0​)​/ℏ. Matching:

A+B=F,k(A−B)=k′F.

Solve:

AB​=k+k′k−k′​,AF​=k+k′2k​.

So

R=(k+k′k−k′​)2.

(c) Transmission coefficient via probability current

The probability current j=(ℏ/m)Im(ψ∗ψ′) for a plane wave ψ=Aeikx is jinc​=ℏk∣A∣2/m on the left and jtr​=ℏk′∣F∣2/m on the right. The transmission coefficient is the ratio of currents,

T=jinc​jtr​​=kk′​​AF​​2=EE−V0​​​​AF​​2,

which is exactly the formula stated in the problem (the prefactor k′/k accounts for the different speeds on the two sides — naive ∣F/A∣2 would be wrong).

(d) Explicit T and the sum rule

Plug in F/A=2k/(k+k′):

T=kk′​⋅(k+k′)24k2​=(k+k′)24kk′​.

Add R:

R+T=(k+k′)2(k−k′)2+4kk′​=(k+k′)2(k+k′)2​=1.✓

Q3. Harmonic oscillator (30 pts)

Define a,a† as in Week 3 so that [a,a†]=1 and H^=ℏω(a†a+1/2).

(i) Wave functions ψ0​,ψ1​,ψ2​

The ground state satisfies aψ0​=0, i.e. (x+(ℏ/mω)∂x​)ψ0​=0. Solving,

ψ0​(x)=(πℏmω​)1/4e−mωx2/(2ℏ).

Using ψn​=(a†)nψ0​/n!​ and the convenient variable ξ=mω/ℏ​x,

ψ1​(x)=(πℏmω​)1/42​ξe−ξ2/2,
ψ2​(x)=(πℏmω​)1/42​1​(2ξ2−1)e−ξ2/2.

These are the Hermite-polynomial wave functions ψn​∝Hn​(ξ)e−ξ2/2.

(ii) Energy spectrum

Apply H^ to ψn​: since a†aψn​=nψn​,

En​=(n+21​)ℏω,n=0,1,2,…​

(iii) Expectation value ⟨E⟩ for ∣Ψ⟩=3​1​(∣0⟩+∣1⟩+∣2⟩)

Since ∣0⟩,∣1⟩,∣2⟩ are orthonormal energy eigenstates,

⟨E⟩=n=0∑2​∣cn​∣2En​=31​(E0​+E1​+E2​)=31​ℏω(21​+23​+25​)=23​ℏω.

Q4. Three-level matrix Hamiltonian (20 pts)

With a,b,c real,

H=​a0b​0c0​b0a​​.

(c) Eigenvalues first (we will need them)

The middle row/column decouples: the canonical basis vector ∣m⟩=(0,1,0)⊤ is already an eigenvector with eigenvalue c. The 2×2 block (ab​ba​) has eigenvalues a±b with normalized eigenvectors

∣+⟩=2​1​​101​​(E=a+b),∣−⟩=2​1​​10−1​​(E=a−b).

(a) Initial state ∣S(0)⟩=(0,1,0)⊤

This is an eigenstate of H with eigenvalue c, so time evolution is just a phase:

∣S(t)⟩=e−ict/ℏ​010​​.

(b) Initial state ∣S(0)⟩=(1,0,0)⊤

Expand in the eigenbasis:

​100​​=2​1​∣+⟩+2​1​∣−⟩.

Evolve each piece by its phase:

∣S(t)⟩=2​1​(e−i(a+b)t/ℏ∣+⟩+e−i(a−b)t/ℏ∣−⟩).

Substitute the explicit vectors and factor out e−iat/ℏ:

∣S(t)⟩=e−iat/ℏ​cos(bt/ℏ)0−isin(bt/ℏ)​​.

Reassuring sanity check: at t=0 we recover (1,0,0)⊤, and the probability oscillates between sites 1 and 3 with angular frequency 2b/ℏ — a textbook Rabi-style oscillation driven by the off-diagonal coupling b.

Common pitfalls

  • Forgetting that the transmission coefficient at a step needs the k′/k prefactor (different speeds).
  • In the finite well, normalizing inside but forgetting the matching condition that fixes the energy.
  • In ⟨E⟩ calculations for a superposition: it is ∑∣cn​∣2En​, not ∑cn​En​.
  • When the Hamiltonian is a matrix, write the initial state in the eigenbasis first, then attach phases. Don't try to exponentiate the matrix directly under time pressure.
