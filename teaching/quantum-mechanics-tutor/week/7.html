Week 7 · Angular momentum & spin (Griffiths Ch. 4.3–4.4)

Spring 2025 · Yonsei PHY3101 — Tutor: Seokwon Choi

Orbital angular momentum: setting the operators

Define L^=r^×p^​, i.e. L^i​=ϵijk​x^j​p^​k​ (Einstein convention; all indices run over 1,2,3). Explicitly,

L^x​=y^​p^​z​−z^p^​y​,L^y​=z^p^​x​−x^p^​z​,L^z​=x^p^​y​−y^​p^​x​.

These are Hermitian (each is a difference of products of commuting operators) and ready to be treated as observables.

Step 1 — Deriving the commutation relations

Start from the canonical [x^i​,p^​j​]=iℏδij​ and the fact that x^i​ commutes with x^j​ and p^​i​ commutes with p^​j​. Compute [L^x​,L^y​] explicitly:

[L^x​,L^y​]=[y^​p^​z​−z^p^​y​,z^p^​x​−x^p^​z​].

Expand the four cross-terms. Three of them vanish; the surviving pieces use only the canonical commutator. The non-zero contribution is

[y^​p^​z​,z^p^​x​]=y^​[p^​z​,z^]p^​x​=−iℏy^​p^​x​,

and

[−z^p^​y​,−x^p^​z​]=x^p^​y​[z^,p^​z​]=iℏx^p^​y​.

The other two pairs commute. So

[L^x​,L^y​]=iℏ(x^p^​y​−y^​p^​x​)=iℏL^z​.

By cyclic symmetry,

[L^i​,L^j​]=iℏϵijk​L^k​.​

Combining this with itself one shows [L^2,L^i​]=0 for any i:

[L^2,L^z​]=[L^x2​+L^y2​,L^z​]=L^x​[L^x​,L^z​]+[L^x​,L^z​]L^x​+(x→y)=−iℏ(L^x​L^y​+L^y​L^x​)+iℏ(L^y​L^x​+L^x​L^y​)=0.

Therefore L^2 and one component (call it L^z​) can be diagonalized simultaneously.

Step 2 — Ladder operators and their algebra

Define

L^±​≡L^x​±iL^y​.

From the basic commutators,

[L^z​,L^±​]=[L^z​,L^x​]±i[L^z​,L^y​]=iℏL^y​±i(−iℏL^x​)=±ℏ(L^x​±iL^y​)=±ℏL^±​,

and

[L^+​,L^−​]=[L^x​+iL^y​,L^x​−iL^y​]=−i[L^x​,L^y​]+i[L^y​,L^x​]=2ℏL^z​.

The first identity says L^+​ raises and L^−​ lowers L^z​ by ℏ: if L^z​∣m⟩=mℏ∣m⟩,

L^z​L^±​∣m⟩=(L^±​L^z​+[L^z​,L^±​])∣m⟩=(mℏ±ℏ)L^±​∣m⟩.

So L^±​∣m⟩∝∣m±1⟩.

Step 3 — The spectrum from the algebra

Pick a simultaneous eigenstate ∣ℓ,m⟩ with L^2∣ℓ,m⟩=λ∣ℓ,m⟩ and L^z​∣ℓ,m⟩=mℏ∣ℓ,m⟩ (we will solve for the form of λ). Since L^x2​+L^y2​=L^2−L^z2​≥0, we have λ≥m2ℏ2. So m is bounded: there is a top state ∣ℓ,mmax​⟩ with L^+​∣ℓ,mmax​⟩=0 and a bottom state ∣ℓ,mmin​⟩ with L^−​∣ℓ,mmin​⟩=0.

Now compute L^−​L^+​ in terms of the Casimir:

L^−​L^+​=(L^x​−iL^y​)(L^x​+iL^y​)=L^x2​+L^y2​+i[L^x​,L^y​]=L^2−L^z2​−ℏL^z​.

Apply to ∣ℓ,mmax​⟩ (LHS gives 0 because L^+​ kills it):

0=λ−mmax2​ℏ2−mmax​ℏ2⟹λ=ℏ2mmax​(mmax​+1).

Same trick with L^+​L^−​=L^2−L^z2​+ℏL^z​ on the bottom state gives

λ=ℏ2mmin​(mmin​−1).

Equating the two and solving the quadratic gives mmin​=−mmax​. Calling ℓ≡mmax​, we read off

λ=ℏ2ℓ(ℓ+1),m∈{−ℓ,−ℓ+1,…,ℓ}.​

The number of steps from −ℓ to +ℓ is an integer, so 2ℓ∈Z≥0​: the algebra alone forces ℓ∈{0,21​,1,23​,…}. For orbital angular momentum we additionally require single-valued wave functions on ϕ→ϕ+2π, killing the half-integers and leaving ℓ=0,1,2,…. Half-integers will reappear for spin.

Step 4 — Ladder amplitudes

We already computed L^−​L^+​=L^2−L^z2​−ℏL^z​. Sandwich on ∣ℓ,m⟩:

∥L^+​∣ℓ,m⟩∥2=⟨ℓ,m∣L^−​L^+​∣ℓ,m⟩=ℏ2[ℓ(ℓ+1)−m2−m]=ℏ2(ℓ−m)(ℓ+m+1).

Take the positive root (standard phase choice):

L^±​∣ℓ,m⟩=ℏ(ℓ∓m)(ℓ±m+1)​∣ℓ,m±1⟩.​

Spin

Spin is an intrinsic angular momentum with no orbital interpretation — it just sits there in addition to L^. Its components obey the same algebra,

[S^i​,S^j​]=iℏϵijk​S^k​,

but now half-integer values s=1/2,3/2,… are allowed (no single-valued-function constraint applies — spin has no spatial wave function). For an electron s=1/2 and the Hilbert space is C2. Standard basis:

∣↑⟩=(10​),∣↓⟩=(01​).

Specializing the ladder identity to ℓ=1/2,m=−1/2 gives S^+​∣↓⟩=ℏ∣↑⟩ and similarly S^−​∣↑⟩=ℏ∣↓⟩. From these,

S^x​=2ℏ​σx​,S^y​=2ℏ​σy​,S^z​=2ℏ​σz​,σ=((01​10​),(0i​−i0​),(10​0−1​)).

Useful identities: σi2​=1, σi​σj​=δij​1+iϵijk​σk​.

Addition of angular momentum (sketch of the recipe)

Couple L^ and S^ into J^=L^+S^. Since the components of L^ commute with those of S^, J^ obeys the same angular-momentum algebra. The product space ∣ℓ,mℓ​⟩⊗∣s,ms​⟩ decomposes into eigenspaces of J^2,J^z​ labelled ∣j,mj​⟩ with

j∈{∣ℓ−s∣,∣ℓ−s∣+1,…,ℓ+s}.

The change-of-basis kernel between ∣ℓ,mℓ​;s,ms​⟩ and ∣j,mj​⟩ is given by the Clebsch–Gordan coefficients (Griffiths Table 4.8). For ℓ=1,s=1/2 we get j∈{1/2,3/2}, i.e. a 6-dim product space splits into 2 + 4. This is exactly the setup for problem 2 of the final.

The identity we will hit hardest in Week 8

From J^2=L^2+2L^⋅S^+S^2,

L^⋅S^=21​(J^2−L^2−S^2).

On a state with definite j,ℓ,s, the eigenvalue is (ℏ2/2)[j(j+1)−ℓ(ℓ+1)−s(s+1)]. Diagonalizing any spin-orbit Hamiltonian is then a one-line plug-in, without ever touching matrix elements — we will use this on the final.
