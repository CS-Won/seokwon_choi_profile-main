Week 2 · Infinite & finite square wells (Griffiths Ch. 2.1–2.2, 2.6)

Spring 2025 · Yonsei PHY3101 — Tutor: Seokwon Choi

From time-dependent to time-independent Schrödinger

When V(x) has no explicit time dependence, try a separable ansatz Ψ(x,t)=ψ(x)T(t) in the time-dependent Schrödinger equation:

iℏψ(x)T′(t)=−2mℏ2​ψ′′(x)T(t)+V(x)ψ(x)T(t).

Divide both sides by ψ(x)T(t):

function of tiℏT(t)T′(t)​​​=function of x−2mℏ2​ψ(x)ψ′′(x)​+V(x)​​.

The two sides depend on different variables, so each must equal a common constant — call it E. The right-hand side then gives the time-independent Schrödinger equation (TISE),

−2mℏ2​ψ′′(x)+V(x)ψ(x)=Eψ(x),

and the left-hand side integrates immediately to

T(t)=e−iEt/ℏ(up to a constant absorbed into ψ).

Stationary states ψn​(x) with energies En​ form a complete set, so a generic wave function is

Ψ(x,t)=n∑​cn​ψn​(x)e−iEn​t/ℏ,cn​=∫ψn∗​(x)Ψ(x,0)dx.

Solve the TISE once; time evolution is then just attaching a phase to each component.

The infinite square well — full derivation

Take V(x)=0 for 0≤x≤L and V(x)=∞ outside. Inside the well the TISE becomes

ψ′′(x)=−k2ψ(x),k≡2mE​/ℏ.

The general real-coefficient solution is ψ(x)=Asin(kx)+Bcos(kx). Outside the well ψ must be zero (anything else gives infinite energy), and the wave function is continuous, so the boundary conditions are ψ(0)=0 and ψ(L)=0.

From ψ(0)=0: Asin0+Bcos0=B=0, so B=0. From ψ(L)=0: Asin(kL)=0. We do not want the trivial solution A=0, so we need sin(kL)=0, i.e. kL=nπ for n=1,2,3,… (the negative n give the same state up to a sign). Solving k=nπ/L for E:

En​=2mℏ2kn2​​=2mL2n2π2ℏ2​.​

Normalize:

∫0L​A2sin2(Lnπx​)dx=A2⋅2L​=1⟹A=L2​​.

So the orthonormal stationary states are

ψn​(x)=L2​​sin(Lnπx​),n=1,2,3,…​

Orthonormality

For m=n,

∫0L​ψm​ψn​dx=L2​∫0L​sin(Lmπx​)sin(Lnπx​)dx=L1​∫0L​[cosL(m−n)πx​−cosL(m+n)πx​]dx=0,

because each cosine integrates to zero over an integer number of half-periods. For m=n we get 1, so ⟨ψm​∣ψn​⟩=δmn​. The completeness relation Ψ(x,0)=∑n​cn​ψn​(x) is then a Fourier sine series, with ∣cn​∣2 the probability of measuring En​.

The finite square well

Now soften the walls: V(x)=−V0​ for ∣x∣≤a (V0​>0), and V(x)=0 outside. Bound states have −V0​<E<0.

Inside the well the TISE is

ψ′′(x)=−k2ψ(x),k=2m(E+V0​)​/ℏ.

Outside,

ψ′′(x)=+κ2ψ(x),κ=−2mE​/ℏ>0,

with exponentially decaying solutions e±κx. The potential is even (V(−x)=V(x)), so the stationary states have definite parity. Take the even states for now.

Even states

Inside, ψin​(x)=Acos(kx) (cosine — keeps the function even). Outside, ψout​(x)=Be−κ∣x∣ (the growing exponential is rejected for normalizability). At x=a require continuity of ψ and ψ′:

Acos(ka)=Be−κa,−Aksin(ka)=−Bκe−κa.

Divide the second equation by the first to eliminate the constants:

ktan(ka)=κ.​

Together with k2+κ2=2mV0​/ℏ2 this fixes the bound-state energies.

Odd states

Inside, ψin​(x)=Asin(kx); outside ψout​(x)=sgn(x)Be−κ∣x∣. Matching at x=a in the same way:

Asin(ka)=Be−κa,Akcos(ka)=−Bκe−κa.

Divide:

−kcot(ka)=κ.​

Graphical / dimensionless form

Introduce z=ka and z0​=(a/ℏ)2mV0​​. From k2+κ2=2mV0​/ℏ2=z02​/a2 we get κa=z02​−z2​. The even condition becomes

ztanz=z02​−z2​,

which has a finite number of solutions for any fixed z0​ — the curves ztanz and z02​−z2​ always cross at least once on [0,z0​], so there is always at least one even bound state, no matter how shallow.

What stays with us

After Week 2 the recipe for any 1D piecewise-constant potential is firmly the same: (i) write the TISE separately on each region; (ii) pick exponential or sinusoidal solutions according to the sign of E−V; (iii) impose decay at infinity for bound states; (iv) match ψ and ψ′ at each boundary; (v) read off the energies. We use this exact procedure for the step and the finite-bottom asymmetric well in the midterm.
