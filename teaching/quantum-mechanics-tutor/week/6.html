Week 6 · Schrödinger equation in 3D — hydrogen (Griffiths Ch. 4.1–4.2)

Spring 2025 · Yonsei PHY3101 — Tutor: Seokwon Choi

Three-dimensional Schrödinger equation

In 3D the kinetic operator is p^​2=−ℏ2∇2 and the wave function is Ψ(r,t). The TISE reads

−2mℏ2​∇2ψ+V(r)ψ=Eψ.

For a central potential V(r)=V(r) (depending only on the distance from the origin), spherical coordinates (r,θ,ϕ) are the natural choice.

Step 1 — The Laplacian in spherical coordinates

A standard computation in vector calculus (chain rule applied to x=rsinθcosϕ etc.) gives

∇2=r21​∂r​(r2∂r​)+r2sinθ1​∂θ​(sinθ∂θ​)+r2sin2θ1​∂ϕ2​.

Group the angular pieces: they assemble into −L^2/(ℏ2r2), where L^2 is the squared angular momentum operator,

L^2=−ℏ2[sinθ1​∂θ​(sinθ∂θ​)+sin2θ1​∂ϕ2​].

So the Laplacian splits cleanly into radial and angular parts:

∇2=r21​∂r​(r2∂r​)−ℏ2r2L^2​.

That is the key algebraic identity for everything that follows.

Step 2 — Separation of variables

Try the product ansatz ψ(r,θ,ϕ)=R(r)Y(θ,ϕ). Substitute into the TISE and multiply both sides by r2/(RY):

depends on r only[R1​∂r​(r2∂r​R)−ℏ22mr2​(V(r)−E)]​​=depends on θ,ϕ onlyℏ2Y1​L^2Y​​.

Both sides equal a constant; following the convention call it ℓ(ℓ+1) (the convenience of this weird choice becomes clear once we solve the angular equation). The angular equation is the eigenvalue problem

L^2Y(θ,ϕ)=ℏ2ℓ(ℓ+1)Y(θ,ϕ),

whose solutions are the spherical harmonics Yℓm​(θ,ϕ) with ℓ=0,1,2,… and m=−ℓ,−ℓ+1,…,+ℓ. (Week 7 derives why this label is half-integer-restricted.)

The radial equation becomes

−2mℏ2​r21​drd​(r2drdR​)+[V(r)+2mr2ℏ2ℓ(ℓ+1)​]R=ER.

The new term ℏ2ℓ(ℓ+1)/(2mr2) is the centrifugal barrier — exactly the analogue of classical orbital angular momentum.

The u(r)=rR(r) substitution

Define u(r)≡rR(r). By the product rule r2R′=ru′−u and (r2R′)′=ru′′, so (1/r2)(r2R′)′=u′′/r. Substituting into the radial equation and multiplying by r,

−2mℏ2​u′′(r)+Veff​(r)u(r)=Eu(r),Veff​(r)≡V(r)+2mr2ℏ2ℓ(ℓ+1)​,

with the boundary condition u(0)=0 (so that R(r)=u(r)/r is regular at the origin). This is a 1D Schrödinger equation on the half-line r≥0 — all our Week 2–3 intuition applies.

Step 3 — Hydrogen

Take V(r)=−e2/(4πϵ0​r) (Coulomb attraction; for clarity drop the constants and call it −Ze2/r with Z=1). Bound states have E<0. Define

κ=ℏ−2mE​​,ρ=κr,ρ0​=2πϵ0​ℏ2κme2​.

The radial equation in u(ρ) becomes

u′′(ρ)=[1−ρρ0​​+ρ2ℓ(ℓ+1)​]u(ρ).

Asymptotics

  • Large ρ: the equation reduces to u′′≈u, so u∼e±ρ. Pick e−ρ for normalizability.
  • Small ρ: the centrifugal term dominates, u′′≈ℓ(ℓ+1)u/ρ2, with solutions u∼ρℓ+1 or ρ−ℓ. Regularity at the origin selects ρℓ+1.

Series solution

Factor out the asymptotic behaviour:

u(ρ)=ρℓ+1e−ρv(ρ).

Substitute into the radial equation. After some algebra,

ρv′′(ρ)+2(ℓ+1−ρ)v′(ρ)+[ρ0​−2(ℓ+1)]v(ρ)=0.

Expand v(ρ)=∑j=0∞​cj​ρj. Plugging in and equating powers of ρj yields the recursion

cj+1​=(j+1)(j+2ℓ+2)2(j+ℓ+1)−ρ0​​cj​.

For large j the recursion ratio is ≈2/j, which corresponds to v∼e2ρ — and would ruin the normalizability of u. Therefore the series must terminate: there must exist some jmax​ with cjmax​+1​=0. From the recursion that means

2(jmax​+ℓ+1)=ρ0​⟹ρ0​=2n,n≡jmax​+ℓ+1∈{1,2,3,…}.

The Bohr formula

Reverse-engineering ρ0​ back to E: from the definitions, ρ0​=me2/(2πϵ0​ℏ2κ)=2n gives κ=me2/(4πϵ0​ℏ2n); then E=−ℏ2κ2/(2m) yields

En​=−2(4πϵ0​)2ℏ2me4​n21​≈−n213.6eV​,n=1,2,3,…​

Quantum numbers and degeneracy

Note that n=jmax​+ℓ+1 forces ℓ≤n−1: the allowed angular-momentum quantum numbers at fixed n are ℓ=0,1,…,n−1. The magnetic quantum number takes 2ℓ+1 values, so the total degeneracy of level n (ignoring spin) is

dn​=ℓ=0∑n−1​(2ℓ+1)=n2.

With spin 21​ this becomes 2n2 — the familiar shell sizes of the periodic table.

Radial probability density

The volume element in spherical coordinates is r2sinθdrdθdϕ. Marginalizing over angles, the probability of finding the electron in the spherical shell [r,r+dr] is

P(r)dr=∣Rnℓ​(r)∣2r2dr=∣unℓ​(r)∣2dr.

Even though ∣R(r)∣2 peaks at the origin for n=1, the r2 factor pushes the peak of P(r) outward. A short calculation shows the most probable radius for the ground state is exactly the Bohr radius a0​=4πϵ0​ℏ2/(me2).
