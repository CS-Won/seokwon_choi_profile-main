Week 5 · Formalism: Hilbert space & operators (Griffiths Ch. 3)

Spring 2025 · Yonsei PHY3101 — Tutor: Seokwon Choi

Rebuilding QM on Hilbert space

Up to Week 3 we treated Ψ(x,t) as a function on the real line. Griffiths Ch. 3 reframes the same physics in an abstract vector space — Hilbert space — where the wave function is just one coordinate representation of a deeper object, the state vector ∣ψ⟩. The point is not abstraction for its own sake: this language is what lets 3D problems, angular momentum, and spin in Weeks 6–7 be tractable, and the same machinery underlies every later course in quantum information.

Hilbert space, axiomatically

A Hilbert space H is:

  • a complex vector space (you can add states and rescale by complex numbers);
  • equipped with an inner product ⟨ϕ∣ψ⟩∈C that is conjugate-linear in the first slot, linear in the second, and satisfies ⟨ψ∣ψ⟩≥0 with equality only for the zero vector;
  • complete under the induced norm ∥ψ∥=⟨ψ∣ψ⟩​ (every Cauchy sequence converges to something in H).

States are unit vectors. The bra–ket notation: ∣ψ⟩ is the ket, ⟨ϕ∣ is the bra (an element of the dual space), and the pairing gives the inner product. In the position representation the inner product is

⟨ϕ∣ψ⟩=∫ϕ∗(x)ψ(x)dx.

Operators and the adjoint

An operator A^:H→H has an adjoint A^† defined by

⟨ϕ∣A^ψ⟩=⟨A^†ϕ∣ψ⟩for all ∣ϕ⟩,∣ψ⟩.

Hermitian: A^†=A^. Unitary: U^†U^=U^U^†=1. Observables are Hermitian, time evolutions are unitary.

The spectral theorem

For a Hermitian operator A^:

  1. all eigenvalues are real;
  2. eigenvectors for distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal: ⟨a∣a′⟩=0 if a=a′;
  3. a complete orthonormal basis of eigenvectors exists, so
    A^=a∑​a∣a⟩⟨a∣,1=a∑​∣a⟩⟨a∣.

Eigenvalues real. If A^∣a⟩=a∣a⟩ with ⟨a∣a⟩=1, then

a=⟨a∣A^∣a⟩=⟨A^a∣a⟩=⟨a∣A^∣a⟩​=aˉ,

so a∈R.

Orthogonality. For A^∣a⟩=a∣a⟩ and A^∣a′⟩=a′∣a′⟩ with a=a′ (both real),

a⟨a′∣a⟩=⟨a′∣A^∣a⟩=⟨A^a′∣a⟩=a′⟨a′∣a⟩,

so ⟨a′∣a⟩=0.

The generalized statistical interpretation

Measuring observable A^ on ∣ψ⟩ yields an eigenvalue a with probability P(a)=∣⟨a∣ψ⟩∣2 and collapses the state to ∣a⟩. Using completeness,

a∑​P(a)=a∑​⟨ψ∣a⟩⟨a∣ψ⟩=⟨ψ∣1∣ψ⟩=1,

and the average is

⟨A^⟩=a∑​aP(a)=a∑​a⟨ψ∣a⟩⟨a∣ψ⟩=⟨ψ∣A^∣ψ⟩.

Time evolution as a unitary

The Schrödinger equation in vector form is iℏdt​∣ψ⟩=H^∣ψ⟩. For a time-independent H^ the formal solution is

∣ψ(t)⟩=U^(t)∣ψ(0)⟩,U^(t)=e−iH^t/ℏ.

Because H^ is Hermitian, U^†U^=e+iHt/ℏe−iHt/ℏ=1, so

⟨ψ(t)∣ψ(t)⟩=⟨ψ(0)∣U^†U^∣ψ(0)⟩=⟨ψ(0)∣ψ(0)⟩.

Hermitian generators give unitary evolutions; that is the abstract reason normalization is preserved (Week 1 derived the same fact from the continuity equation).

The generalized uncertainty principle — full derivation

We rederive the uncertainty bound in operator form. Let A^,B^ be two Hermitian operators and ∣ψ⟩ a unit vector. Shift to the mean-zero operators

A^0​=A^−⟨A^⟩,B^0​=B^−⟨B^⟩,

and define

∣f⟩=A^0​∣ψ⟩,∣g⟩=B^0​∣ψ⟩.

Then

⟨f∣f⟩=⟨ψ∣A^02​∣ψ⟩=σA2​,⟨g∣g⟩=σB2​.

Cauchy–Schwarz. For any two vectors in a Hilbert space,

⟨f∣f⟩⟨g∣g⟩≥∣⟨f∣g⟩∣2.

Standard one-line proof: consider ∣f⟩−(⟨g∣f⟩/⟨g∣g⟩)∣g⟩, compute its squared norm, and use that it is non-negative.

Apply this to the f,g above:

σA2​σB2​≥∣⟨f∣g⟩∣2=∣⟨ψ∣A^0​B^0​∣ψ⟩∣2.

Now use the fact that any complex number satisfies ∣z∣2≥(Imz)2:

∣⟨f∣g⟩∣2≥(Im⟨f∣g⟩)2=(2i1​[⟨f∣g⟩−⟨g∣f⟩])2.

The bracket is

⟨f∣g⟩−⟨g∣f⟩=⟨ψ∣A^0​B^0​−B^0​A^0​∣ψ⟩=⟨ψ∣[A^0​,B^0​]∣ψ⟩=⟨ψ∣[A^,B^]∣ψ⟩,

because the c-number shifts in A^0​,B^0​ drop out of the commutator. Combining,

σA2​σB2​≥(2i1​⟨[A^,B^]⟩)2.​

With A^=x^,B^=p^​ and [x^,p^​]=iℏ,

σx​σp​≥2ℏ​.

Equality requires both inequalities above to be saturated: the Cauchy–Schwarz one ((∣f⟩ proportional to ∣g⟩) and the "Im part = the whole thing" one (their inner product is purely imaginary). Solving these gives Gaussian wave packets.

Position and momentum representations

Position eigenstates are generalized (delta-normalized) vectors defined by x^∣x⟩=x∣x⟩. The position-space wave function is ψ(x)=⟨x∣ψ⟩. Momentum eigenstates similarly satisfy p^​∣p⟩=p∣p⟩; we can derive the change-of-basis kernel.

Act p^​ on ⟨x∣p⟩ in position space, using p^​→−iℏ∂x​:

−iℏ∂x​⟨x∣p⟩=p⟨x∣p⟩⟹⟨x∣p⟩=Ceipx/ℏ.

The constant C=1/2πℏ​ is fixed by the delta-normalization ⟨p∣p′⟩=δ(p−p′) and the Fourier identity ∫ei(p−p′)x/ℏdx=2πℏδ(p−p′). So

ψ~​(p)≡⟨p∣ψ⟩=∫⟨p∣x⟩⟨x∣ψ⟩dx=2πℏ​1​∫e−ipx/ℏψ(x)dx,

which is exactly the Fourier transform. Position-space and momentum-space wave functions are just different bases for the same ∣ψ⟩.

Why this matters next

In Weeks 6–7 the natural label for a state is ∣n,ℓ,m⟩ (energy, angular momentum squared, projection). Spelling out ψ(r,θ,ϕ) in that eigenbasis is short; trying to handle everything in real-space PDEs without the Hilbert-space picture is painful. The same comment applies to spin in Week 7: a 2-dim Hilbert space with the Pauli algebra is the entire story.
