Week 1 · The wave function (Griffiths Ch. 1)

Spring 2025 · Yonsei PHY3101 — Tutor: Seokwon Choi

Why a wave function?

Classical mechanics describes a particle by x(t) and p(t). Quantum mechanics replaces this by a single complex-valued function Ψ(x,t) — the wave function — which contains everything we can predict about the particle. The goal of Griffiths Ch. 1 is to make that vague claim precise: state the equation that governs Ψ, the rule that turns it into probabilities, and the constraints needed for the probabilities to make sense.

The Schrödinger equation

The wave function evolves under the time-dependent Schrödinger equation:

iℏ∂t∂Ψ​=−2mℏ2​∂x2∂2Ψ​+V(x,t)Ψ.

Writing the Hamiltonian H^=p^​2/(2m)+V(x^) with p^​=−iℏ∂x​ and x^ψ(x)=xψ(x), the same equation is

iℏ∂t​Ψ=H^Ψ.

Because the equation is first order in t, specifying Ψ(x,0) determines Ψ(x,t) for all later times. A small sanity check: dimensionally, [ℏ/t]=[ℏ2/(mx2)]=energy, so each term is an energy density times Ψ, as required.

The Born rule

The squared modulus is interpreted as a probability density:

ρ(x,t)≡∣Ψ(x,t)∣2=Ψ∗(x,t)Ψ(x,t),

so the probability of finding the particle in [a,b] at time t is ∫ab​∣Ψ∣2dx. The only way this can be sensible is if the total is 1:

∫−∞∞​∣Ψ(x,t)∣2dx=1(normalization).

Step 1 — Probability conservation: full derivation

We need to check that if Ψ is normalized at one moment, the Schrödinger equation keeps it normalized for ever. Differentiate ρ=Ψ∗Ψ:

∂t∂ρ​=∂t∂Ψ∗​Ψ+Ψ∗∂t∂Ψ​.

From the Schrödinger equation ∂t​Ψ=(iℏ/(2m))∂x2​Ψ−(i/ℏ)VΨ. Its complex conjugate is ∂t​Ψ∗=−(iℏ/(2m))∂x2​Ψ∗+(i/ℏ)VΨ∗ (the V term is real, so it changes sign). Substitute:

∂t∂ρ​=−2miℏ​(∂x2​Ψ∗)Ψ+2miℏ​Ψ∗(∂x2​Ψ)+=0ℏi​VΨ∗Ψ−ℏi​VΨ∗Ψ​​.

The potential drops out — that is the punchline. The two surviving terms can be combined using the product rule ∂x​(Ψ∗∂x​Ψ−Ψ∂x​Ψ∗)=Ψ∗∂x2​Ψ−Ψ∂x2​Ψ∗:

∂t∂ρ​=2miℏ​∂x​(Ψ∗∂x​Ψ−Ψ∂x​Ψ∗).

Define the probability current

j(x,t)≡2miℏ​(Ψ∗∂x​Ψ−Ψ∂x​Ψ∗)=mℏ​Im(Ψ∗∂x​Ψ).

Then we have the continuity equation

∂t∂ρ​+∂x∂j​=0.​

Integrating over all x and using Ψ→0 at infinity (any normalizable function must decay there) kills j at the boundary, so

dtd​∫−∞∞​∣Ψ(x,t)∣2dx=−[j(x,t)]−∞+∞​=0.

Normalize once at t=0, normalized for ever.

Step 2 — Expectation values and momentum representation

For an observable represented by an operator A^ we define

⟨A^⟩≡∫−∞∞​Ψ∗(x,t)A^Ψ(x,t)dx.

In particular ⟨x⟩=∫Ψ∗xΨdx. The corresponding formula for the average momentum is not ∫Ψ∗m(dx/dt)Ψ — there is no operator d/dt on the wave function. Instead we proceed by computing d⟨x⟩/dt and reading off the right operator.

How p^​=−iℏ∂x​ appears

Differentiate ⟨x⟩:

dtd⟨x⟩​=∫x∂t∂∣Ψ∣2​dx=−∫x∂x∂j​dx,

using the continuity equation. Integrate by parts (the boundary term vanishes) and substitute j:

dtd⟨x⟩​=∫jdx=∫2miℏ​(Ψ∗∂x​Ψ−Ψ∂x​Ψ∗)dx.

Integrate the second term by parts so both terms have Ψ∗ on the left:

∫Ψ∂x​Ψ∗dx=−∫Ψ∗∂x​Ψdx⟹dtd⟨x⟩​=m1​∫Ψ∗(−iℏ∂x​)Ψdx.

Comparing with the classical m⟨x⟩˙​=⟨p⟩ forces us to identify

p^​=−iℏ∂x∂​.​

Step 3 — Ehrenfest's theorem

For any operator A^ that does not depend explicitly on time,

dtd⟨A^⟩​=∫[(∂t​Ψ∗)A^Ψ+Ψ∗A^(∂t​Ψ)]dx.

Replace ∂t​Ψ by (1/iℏ)H^Ψ and use H^†=H^ on the conjugate:

dtd⟨A^⟩​=iℏ1​⟨[A^,H^]⟩(Ehrenfest).

Specializing to A^=x^ with H^=p^​2/(2m)+V and using [x^,p^​2]=2iℏp^​ recovers m⟨x⟩˙​=⟨p^​⟩. Taking A^=p^​ and [p^​,V]=−iℏV′(x) gives

dtd⟨p^​⟩​=−⟨V′(x^)⟩,

the quantum analogue of Newton's second law (Ehrenfest's theorem). The averages obey the classical equations of motion — but the right-hand side is ⟨V′(x^)⟩, not V′(⟨x^⟩), which is why quantum dynamics differs from classical for wide wave packets.

Step 4 — The position–momentum uncertainty principle

Define standard deviations σA2​=⟨(A^−⟨A^⟩)2⟩. A short derivation in Week 5 (Cauchy–Schwarz on the inner product) shows

σx​σp​≥21​∣⟨[x^,p^​]⟩∣=2ℏ​.

For now, the canonical commutator is the key ingredient. Compute it directly on a test function f(x):

(x^p^​−p^​x^)f=−iℏ[xf′−(xf)′]=−iℏ[xf′−f−xf′]=iℏf.

So

[x^,p^​]=iℏ.​

Equality in the uncertainty inequality is achieved by Gaussian wave packets — we will check that explicitly in Week 3.

Road map

Weeks 2–3 solve the Schrödinger equation for the textbook 1D potentials. Week 5 rebuilds the same machinery in Hilbert-space language. Weeks 6–7 go to 3D, hydrogen, and angular momentum. Weeks 4 and 8 are exam-style problem solving.
