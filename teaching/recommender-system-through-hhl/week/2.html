Week 2 · Recommendation problem & classical tools

Winter 2024 · QISCA — Lecturer: Seokwon Choi

Before the quantum algorithm, classical researchers had already figured out how to get good low-rank approximations fast by sampling entries or rows instead of computing the full SVD on a giant matrix. This week we walk through the math step by step. These are exactly the tools Tang later mimics in the quantum-inspired classical algorithm.

1. Singular value decomposition — a quick refresher

Any real m×n matrix A has a singular value decomposition

A=UΣV⊤=j=1∑r​σj​uj​vj⊤​

where r=rank(A), the σj​≥0 are the singular values in decreasing order, and {uj​},{vj​} are orthonormal left and right singular vectors. Geometrically: every linear map is rotate → stretch along axes → rotate.

The Frobenius norm has a clean expression in terms of singular values:

∥A∥F2​=i,j∑​Aij2​=j=1∑r​σj2​

2. The Eckart–Young theorem — best rank-k approximation

Truncate the sum after k terms:

Ak​=j=1∑k​σj​uj​vj⊤​

Eckart–Young (1936) says this is the best you can do in Frobenius norm:

rank(D)≤kmin​∥A−D∥F2​=∥A−Ak​∥F2​=j>k∑​σj2​

One-line intuition: any rank-k matrix has at most k nonzero singular values, so when you write ∥A−D∥F2​=∑j​σj2​ and apply Mirsky's inequality, you cannot beat zeroing out the top k directions of A itself.

Practically the catch is cost: exact SVD scales as O(mnmin(m,n)). For Netflix-sized A, infeasible.

3. FKV — Monte Carlo low-rank approximation

Frieze, Kannan, and Vempala's trick is: build a small matrix that carries the same singular structure, then SVD that small matrix. Their sampling rule uses squared row norms as probabilities.

Define

pi​=∥A∥F2​∥Ai,⋅​∥2​,i∑​pi​=1

and similarly column probabilities qj​=∥A⋅,j​∥2/∥A∥F2​. The recipe:

  1. Sample s row indices i1​,…,is​ i.i.d. from p. Form the s×n matrix R by stacking the sampled rows, each rescaled by 1/spit​​​. This rescaling is what makes the estimator unbiased.
  2. Sample s columns from R by row-norms of R, to get an s×s matrix W (constant size!).
  3. Compute the top-k singular vectors of W classically — cheap, because W is just s×s.
  4. Lift those singular vectors back to A via a simple matrix–vector product, getting an approximate top-k basis.

The key unbiasedness identity behind this works in expectation:

E[R⊤R]=i∑​pi​⋅pi​Ai,⋅⊤​Ai,⋅​​=i∑​Ai,⋅⊤​Ai,⋅​=A⊤A

So R⊤R is an unbiased estimator of A⊤A, and the eigenvectors of A⊤A are exactly the right singular vectors of A. Concentration inequalities then say that for s=O(k2/ε4) samples we get, with high probability, a rank-k approximationA~k​ with

∥A−A~k​∥F2​≤rank(D)≤kmin​∥A−D∥F2​+ε∥A∥F2​

The total time is polynomial in k,1/ε plus the cost of reading the sampled rows. The matrix size m,n only appears under sampling access — you don't touch most of A (Frieze, Kannan, & Vempala, 2004).

4. Achlioptas–McSherry — entrywise sparsification

A different angle: zero out most entries of A randomly, scale the survivors so the expectation is preserved, and then run a low-rank approximation on the sparser matrix.

Sample each entry with probability p:

A~ij​={Aij​/p,0,​with prob p,otherwise.​

Define the noise N=A~−A. By construction E[Nij​]=0 and Var(Nij​)≤Aij2​(1−p)/p. Random matrix theory says the operator norm ∥N∥2​ stays small with high probability — roughly the size of a random matrix with similar entries — while the large singular values of A survive intact. Then a perturbation bound (Weyl-type) gives

∣σj​(A~)−σj​(A)∣≤∥N∥2​for all j

and consequently the low-rank approximation built from A~ is nearly as good as the one built from A (Achlioptas & McSherry, 2007). Moral: if your matrix has clear "signal" (a few large σj​), you can throw away most of it and still see the signal.

5. Why ℓ²-norm sampling matters (preview of Tang)

Both FKV and Achlioptas–McSherry use ℓ²-norm sampling: probabilities proportional to Aij2​, ∥Ai,⋅​∥2, or ∥A⋅,j​∥2. Why squared? Because the Frobenius norm distributes over squares of singular values:

∥A∥F2​=j∑​σj2​

So sampling weighted by (entry magnitudes)2 is the classical analogue of quantum amplitudes (which also sit under squares in the Born rule). Tang (2019) makes this analogy precise — same ℓ2-norm primitives, no quantum amplitudes needed.

6. Competitive guarantees (back to DKR)

Tying it together with Week 0: Drineas, Kerenidis, and Raghavan compare algorithms that reconstruct big chunks of A to ones that target high-utility entries directly. Their competitive ratio is, informally,

utility of the omniscient top-ℓ listE[utility of our top-ℓ list]​≥1−ε

and they show this is achievable from a small, random subset of revealed entries (Drineas, Kerenidis, & Raghavan, 2002). This means we don't need full reconstruction — exactly the point both quantum and quantum-inspired algorithms exploit.
