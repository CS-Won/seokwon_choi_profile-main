Week 9 · Shor's algorithm

Winter 2024 · Yonsei Premium Online Lecture — Lecturer: Seokwon Choi

Shor's algorithm is the headline result of quantum computing: it factors integers in polynomial time. Since RSA encryption is built on the assumption that factoring is hard, Shor's algorithm is roughly "the reason cryptographers are nervous about quantum computers". Let's see how it works.

1. RSA in 60 seconds

Pick two big primes p,q, multiply them to get N=pq, and publish N. The hard step for an eavesdropper: given only N, recover p and q.

The RSA pieces:

  • Public key: (N,e) with gcd(e,φ(N))=1.
  • Private key: d with ed≡1(modφ(N)), where φ(N)=(p−1)(q−1).
  • Encryption: c=memodN.
  • Decryption: m=cdmodN.

Toy example: p=17,q=19 → N=323,φ(N)=288. Pick e=7; then d=247. Plaintext m=65 encrypts to c=657mod323=255, and 255247mod323 brings us back to 65.

Classically, factoring N by brute force is exponential in the number of digits, so for a 2048-bit RSA modulus we currently say "impossible". Shor's algorithm changes that to polynomial.

2. The reduction: factoring → period finding

Pick a random a with gcd(a,N)=1 and consider the function

f(x)=axmodN

This is periodic in x: f(x+r)=f(x) for some period r. If we find that r and it's even and ar/2≡−1(modN), then

ar≡1(modN)⇒(ar/2−1)(ar/2+1)≡0(modN)

Compute gcd(ar/2±1,N) with the Euclidean algorithm and out pop p and q. So factoring reduces to finding the period of axmodN. Classically, that period finding is still exponential. Quantumly, it isn't.

3. Quantum period finding

  1. Use 2n qubits for the "counting" register, where n=⌈log2​N⌉, and another n qubits to hold axmodN.
  2. Hadamard the counting register so it holds the uniform superposition ∑x​∣x⟩.
  3. Apply the modular exponentiation oracle Ua​∣x⟩∣0⟩=∣x⟩∣axmodN⟩.
  4. Apply inverse QFT to the counting register, then measure it.

After step 3 the state is ∑x​∣x⟩∣axmodN⟩. Measuring the second register would collapse the first to a periodic superposition ∑l​∣x0​+lr⟩ for some random x0​. The QFT of that comb is another comb, but with spacing 22n/r — so its peaks tell you r indirectly.

4. Pulling out the period — continued fractions

What we measure is some k that's roughly

22nk​≈rs​for some integer s

We don't know s or r, but a classical continued-fraction expansion of k/22n recovers candidate fractions s′/r′. For each candidate, check whether ar′≡1(modN). If yes, we've got the period.

5. Putting it all together

  1. Pick random a with gcd(a,N)=1.
  2. Use Shor's quantum subroutine to find the period r of axmodN.
  3. If r is odd or ar/2≡−1(modN), pick a new a.
  4. Otherwise compute p=gcd(ar/2−1,N),q=gcd(ar/2+1,N).

With high probability, a constant number of tries finds the period and thus the factorization. Total runtime: polynomial in logN — versus exponential classically.

6. Why this matters

  • RSA-2048 keys are estimated to need roughly ∼4,000 logical qubits and many billion gates to break with Shor. We're not there yet on real hardware, but it's now a roadmap problem, not a wall.
  • NIST has already standardized post-quantum cryptography alternatives (lattice-based, hash-based) that resist Shor.

Recap

  • RSA security ⇐ factoring is hard ⇐ period finding is hard.
  • Period finding becomes easy with QFT, which gives the quantum speedup.
  • Shor: O(log3N) quantum operations vs exp(logN) classical.
