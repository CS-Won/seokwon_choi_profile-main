Week 3 · Math for quantum computing (2)

Winter 2024 · Yonsei Premium Online Lecture — Lecturer: Seokwon Choi

Last week was abstract linear algebra. This week we tie all of that directly to a physical qubit. We'll see what a qubit state actually looks like, why the Bloch sphere is the right picture, how multi-qubit states work, and what changes for mixed states (which is what real devices give you).

1. How does a qubit store information?

In a classical bit, you pick a physical thing with two stable states: a light bulb that is on (= 1) or off (= 0). For a qubit, you need a system that can sit in both at once with complex amplitudes. A great pedagogical example is the polarization of a photon.

Send unpolarized light through a vertical polarizer and only the vertical component comes out — call that state ∣0⟩. Add a second polarizer at 90°, and nothing makes it through. Tilt the second polarizer by some angle θ and a fraction cos2θ survives. That fraction is exactly the Born rule for a qubit in state cos(θ)∣0⟩+sin(θ)∣1⟩.

2. Normalization

A general single-qubit pure state is

∣ψ⟩=α∣0⟩+β∣1⟩,α,β∈C

and the probabilities of measuring 0 or 1 must add up to 1:

∣α∣2+∣β∣2=1,⟨ψ∣ψ⟩=1

So α,β are complex numbers constrained to a unit sphere in C2.

3. Complex numbers, in five lines

A complex number is c=a+bi with i2=−1. The conjugate is c∗=a−bi, and the modulus is

∣c∣=c∗c​=a2+b2​

Euler's identity gives us the polar form:

eiθ=cosθ+isinθ,c=∣c∣eiargc

4. Phase, global phase, and the Bloch sphere

Using normalization and Euler's identity, we can rewrite a general qubit state as:

∣ψ⟩=eiγ(cos2θ​∣0⟩+eiφsin2θ​∣1⟩)

The factor eiγ is called the global phase. It doesn't change any measurement probability, so we usually drop it. What remains is just (θ,φ), two real numbers between 0 and π / 2π. Those are the angles on the Bloch sphere.

Single-qubit gates are just rotations on this sphere. Two-qubit gates can't be drawn on a single Bloch sphere — they entangle two spheres into a 4-dimensional space.

5. Multi-qubit states

For two qubits we take a tensor product of single-qubit spaces:

∣ψ⟩=ij∈{0,1}∑​cij​∣ij⟩,∣c00​∣2+∣c01​∣2+∣c10​∣2+∣c11​∣2=1

Some two-qubit states factor cleanly as ∣ψ⟩=∣ψ1​⟩⊗∣ψ2​⟩. Others don't. The famous example is the Bell state:

∣Φ+⟩=2​1​(∣00⟩+∣11⟩)

If we try to write ∣Φ+⟩=(a∣0⟩+b∣1⟩)⊗(c∣0⟩+d∣1⟩), we get four conditions ac=bd=1/2​,ad=bc=0 that can't all be satisfied at once. So the Bell state cannot be written as a product — that's the formal definition of entanglement.

6. Pure vs mixed states

Everything above assumed our state vector is known exactly — that's a pure state. Real devices give you mixed states: classical probabilities over different pure states.

The cleanest object to describe both at once is the density operator. For a pure state it's just ρ=∣ψ⟩⟨ψ∣. For a classical mixture with probabilities pi​ of being in state ∣ψi​⟩:

ρ=i∑​pi​∣ψi​⟩⟨ψi​∣

Key properties:

  • Hermitian: ρ†=ρ.
  • Trace 1: Tr(ρ)=1 (probabilities sum to 1).
  • Positive semi-definite: all eigenvalues ≥0.
  • Purity check: Tr(ρ2)=1 iff the state is pure; Tr(ρ2)<1 means mixed.

Density matrices are the language used for noisy hardware, decoherence, and the entanglement-entropy measurements we sometimes care about. We won't need the full machinery until later, but it's good to meet it once.

Recap

  • Pure single-qubit state: parametrized by (θ,φ) on the Bloch sphere.
  • Global phase doesn't matter; relative phase does.
  • Multi-qubit states live in tensor-product spaces; entangled states can't be factored.
  • Density matrix ρ=∑i​pi​∣ψi​⟩⟨ψi​∣ handles both pure and mixed cases uniformly.
