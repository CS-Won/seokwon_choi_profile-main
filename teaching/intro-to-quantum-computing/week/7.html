Week 7 · Quantum Fourier transform (2)

Winter 2024 · Yonsei Premium Online Lecture — Lecturer: Seokwon Choi

Now we go from continuous Fourier transforms to the discrete version a computer can actually run, and then to the quantum version — the QFT. The cool thing is that the QFT is exactly the same matrix as the classical DFT; the speedup comes from being able to apply that matrix much faster on a quantum register.

1. Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT)

Take a signal sampled at N points x0​,x1​,…,xN−1​. The DFT gives you N frequency coefficients:

yk​=N​1​j=0∑N−1​xj​e−2πijk/N,k=0,1,…,N−1

That's a linear transformation with matrix entries Fkj​=N​1​e−2πijk/N. Done naively it costs O(N2) arithmetic operations. The classical Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) brings this down to O(NlogN) by recursively splitting even and odd indices.

2. Quantum Fourier Transform — the definition

We just promote the DFT to act on quantum states. Suppose we have n qubits, so N=2n basis states. The QFT is the unitary:

QFT∣j⟩=N​1​k=0∑N−1​e2πijk/N∣k⟩

And on a general superposition:

QFT(j∑​xj​∣j⟩)=k∑​yk​∣k⟩,yk​=N​1​j∑​xj​e2πijk/N

Same coefficients as the classical DFT, but they live in theamplitudes of a quantum state. Whether we can read out those amplitudes is a separate question — for most algorithms we just care that some clever measurement on the QFT-transformed state gives the answer we want.

3. The QFT circuit

The clever decomposition uses Hadamards and controlled phase rotations:

Rk​=(10​0e2πi/2k​)

Here's how the QFT acts on the basis state ∣j1​j2​…jn​⟩:

QFT∣j1​j2​…jn​⟩=2n​1​l=1⨂n​(∣0⟩+e2πi0.jl​jl+1​…jn​∣1⟩)

Here 0.jl​jl+1​…jn​ means the binary fraction jl​/2+jl+1​/4+…. Each output qubit is a Hadamarded state with an extra phase that depends on all the lower input qubits — which is exactly what a chain of controlled phase rotations gives you.

The pattern: on each qubit, apply Hadamard, then apply controlled-R2​,R3​,… from every qubit below. After all qubits are processed, a SWAP layer reverses their order to match the textbook definition.

4. Gate count and the speedup

Counting up: n Hadamards plus, for each qubit, up to n−1 controlled phases. Total:

#gates∼2n(n+1)​=O(n2)=O(log2N)

Compare to classical FFT (O(NlogN)) and classical DFT (O(N2)). The QFT is exponentially faster, with the giant caveat that you can't read all N coefficients out; you only get to sample. Algorithms like Shor's are designed so that one sample is all you need.

AlgorithmCost
DFTO(N²)
FFTO(N log N)
QFTO(log² N) = O(n²)

5. Sanity check: 2-qubit QFT explicitly

For n=2, the QFT matrix is

F4​=21​​1111​1i−1−i​1−11−1​1−i−1i​​

And the circuit version is just Hadamard on qubit 0, controlled-R2​ from qubit 1 to qubit 0, Hadamard on qubit 1, then SWAP. Multiplying those four matrices gives F4​. Same unitary, just built out of one- and two-qubit gates.

Recap

  • QFT = DFT in amplitudes: ∣j⟩→N​1​∑k​e2πijk/N∣k⟩.
  • Circuit: Hadamards + controlled phase rotations + SWAPs.
  • Gate count O(log2N) vs classical O(NlogN).
  • The exponential speedup is real, but you only get to sample the output. Algorithms must be designed with that in mind.
